## Assistant

Great question — let me explain why they exist and whether they're actually necessary.

## What reverse bigram indices do

The **reverse bigram indices** answer: "given that the first token of my suffix is `s₁`, which candidate predecessor tokens `v` are most likely?"

Normally the bigram matrix is stored as `P(w | v)` — "given token v, what comes next?" But for backward sampling, we need the reverse: "given that `w = s₁` came next, which `v` most likely preceded it?" That's looking up **column** `s₁` of the bigram matrix and sorting by value.

The precomputed reverse indices store these sorted orders so that at sampling time, we iterate candidates in a smart order (most promising first) rather than randomly. This is what makes the **nucleus stopping criterion** efficient — we find enough probability mass quickly and stop early, instead of scoring all 50k tokens.

## Do we actually need to store them?

Honestly, **maybe not as a separate artifact**. CSC (Compressed Sparse Column) format lets you efficiently access columns directly from the sparse matrix. So instead of precomputing and storing 200 MB of indices, we could:
1. Store the bigram matrix in **both CSR and CSC** format (CSC is just a transpose)
2. At sampling time, extract column `s₁` from CSC and argsort on the fly

The argsort of ~2,000-5,000 nonzero entries takes microseconds. This would save ~200 MB of storage at negligible runtime cost.
